## 1. Executive Summary

This case study analyzes a remote ERP onboarding experience in which a new employee was exposed to live project artifacts, customer-specific implementation materials, formal ERP training, manufacturing ERP concepts, data reconciliation tasks, translation work, presentation assignments, and self-study materials during the early stage of employment.

The central finding is that the difficulty of the onboarding experience cannot be explained simply as a lack of effort, a lack of intelligence, or a lack of isolated training materials. The stronger explanation is **workflow scaffolding failure**.

The onboarding environment included many materials and learning opportunities, but the relationship among those materials was not sufficiently staged. Training, customer work, artifact interpretation, role expectations, feedback, access boundaries, and operational responsibilities were compressed into one remote learning context.

This report interprets the case through the following frame:

> Remote ERP onboarding failed when live project delivery, artifact management, role expectation, knowledge transfer, and learner progress were not organized into a staged workflow.

From a CWI perspective, the case is useful because it shows how organizational confusion can be converted into structured analytical data. The case therefore becomes not only a personal work-history artifact, but also a foundation for **Combinatorial Workflow Intelligence**: the analysis of organizational work as combinations of people, documents, systems, events, constraints, roles, and outcomes.

## 2. Project Purpose

The purpose of this project is to convert a difficult remote ERP onboarding experience into a structured workflow case study.

The project has five goals.

1. Document the onboarding structure without exposing confidential materials.
2. Classify the failure patterns using an onboarding failure taxonomy.
3. Translate fragmented artifacts into analyzable workflow signals.
4. Build public-safe synthetic and reconstructed data for GitHub-based portfolio demonstration.
5. Connect the case to SAP-style CWI and future operational intelligence research.

This project does not claim that every difficulty came from one actor or one decision. Instead, it argues that the difficulty emerged from the interaction of many components: remote work, ERP complexity, customer-specific artifacts, fragmented documents, unclear role boundaries, weak feedback loops, and early live project exposure.

---

## 3. Research Questions

This report is organized around the following questions.

1. What happens when a new employee is exposed to live ERP project complexity before basic workflow scaffolding is established?
2. How do fragmented documents, meeting notes, customer files, videos, spreadsheets, and self-study artifacts increase onboarding burden?
3. How can a difficult onboarding experience be translated into event-log-style data?
4. How can role ambiguity be detected through artifact and activity patterns?
5. How can the case inform future ERP onboarding design, SAP-style CWI, and operational intelligence systems?

## 5. Methodology

This case study uses a qualitative-to-structured workflow analysis method.

### Step 1: Artifact inventory

Original material categories were reviewed and grouped into public-safe artifact categories such as meeting summaries, training references, sales order upload materials, manufacturing ERP materials, data reconciliation files, translation assignments, and self-study files.

### Step 2: Timeline reconstruction

The onboarding period was reconstructed as a monthly sequence from January 2023 to September 2023. The timeline does not reproduce confidential contents. It reconstructs the learning and work burden by describing which types of activities appeared in each period.

### Step 3: Event-log-style modeling

The case was converted into a reconstructed event log with fields such as `event_date`, `phase_month`, `activity_type`, `domain`, `artifact_category`, `role_mode`, `learning_stage`, `complexity_level`, `onboarding_risk_tags`, and `cwi_signal`.

This design imitates a process-mining mindset, but the dataset is reconstructed and public-safe rather than extracted from a production ERP system.

### Step 4: Failure taxonomy

Observed patterns were converted into reusable failure types, including live project exposure before scaffolding, artifact fragmentation, role ambiguity, domain overload, self-onboarding burden, and security/access boundary risk.

### Step 5: Role expectation matrix

The learner’s changing role modes were modeled as a matrix. The project distinguishes learner, observer, note-taker, translator, presenter, data checker, manual author, manufacturing ERP trainee, self-onboarding manager, and project support person.

### Step 6: CWI interpretation

The final step interprets the case as a CWI problem: a failure to align context, work, impact, artifacts, roles, and feedback into one coherent onboarding system.

## 6. Chronological Case Reconstruction

### 6.1 January 2023: Live project exposure before structured onboarding

January represents the first major failure pattern. The onboarding environment exposed the new employee to live customer implementation artifacts almost immediately.

The materials involved customer meetings, NetSuite integration references, e-commerce and marketplace systems, WMS/logistics concepts, return/RMA processes, field mapping, access-related references, production/inventory materials, BOM topics, accounting responses, and manufacturing-related questions.

The core issue is that the employee was not simply learning ERP basics. The employee was trying to infer ERP structure from live project fragments.

**Interpretation:** January was not a beginner onboarding month. It was a live project exposure month.

### 6.2 February 2023: Compressed ERP consultant enablement

February introduced formal training and additional work tracks. However, the presence of formal training did not solve the onboarding problem because training, customer artifacts, presentation assignments, translation tasks, and manufacturing ERP concepts were compressed together.

The learner encountered D&D/delivery training, import and data migration exercises, O2C alignment presentation materials, payment help translation, BOM and Work Order training, outsourced manufacturing concepts, and self-study files.

**Interpretation:** February shows that the problem was not total absence of education. The problem was excessive compression and weak sequencing.

### 6.3 March 2023: Artifact and meeting fragmentation

March expanded the scope into delivery, workflow, return, approval, intercompany, inventory, MRP, and point lessons.

At this stage, the evidence suggests that the learner was exposed to more project activities and meeting summaries, but still lacked a single stable map of how all artifacts related to learning goals and role expectations.

**Interpretation:** March shows artifact and meeting fragmentation. The employee had many signals but insufficient hierarchy.

### 6.4 April–May 2023: Data and process deep dive

April and May show a transition from learning to operational output.

The materials involved sales order upload, return processing, external system comparisons, NetSuite and marketplace data reconciliation, error lists, upload manuals, and multi-system data quality checks.

A notable pattern is the shift from receiving materials to producing workflow artifacts. The employee was no longer only learning; the employee was helping document, compare, or interpret operational process data.

**Interpretation:** April–May shows role escalation. The learner became a data checker, manual author, and operational support person.

### 6.5 June–July 2023: Manufacturing and ERP fragmentation

June and July deepened the manufacturing ERP burden.

The materials involved outsourced manufacturing, Phantom settings, BOM, Routing, Work Order, factory process interviews, production planning, purchasing, raw materials, costing, and manufacturing-related ERP uploads.

These concepts require prior understanding of master data, transaction logic, accounting impact, and production workflow.

**Interpretation:** June–July shows manufacturing ERP fragmentation. The learning environment expanded into production, inventory, purchasing, and costing at the same time.

### 6.6 August–September 2023: Failure realization and reconstruction

By August and September, the case had accumulated multiple layers: live project exposure, customer artifacts, meeting notes, manufacturing ERP data, upload files, cost logic, reporting templates, missing material lists, and self-study records.

This stage can be interpreted as the realization that the onboarding experience had not been an ordinary learning path. It was a compressed and fragmented workflow environment.

**Interpretation:** August–September becomes the point where the experience can be reconstructed as a case study rather than only remembered as a personal failure.

## 8. Key Findings

### Finding 1: Exposure was mistaken for onboarding

The case shows a common but dangerous assumption: if a new employee is exposed to enough meetings, documents, videos, and customer artifacts, learning will naturally occur.

Exposure provides information, but onboarding requires sequence, context, practice, feedback, and role boundaries.

### Finding 2: The learner had to design the learning system

The presence of self-study notes, keyword files, and personal reconstruction efforts suggests that the employee had to create a private learning pathway.

Self-directed learning is valuable, but when self-directed learning replaces organizational scaffolding, the company transfers onboarding design burden to the learner.

### Finding 3: Role boundaries shifted too early

The employee moved across several roles: learner, observer, translator, note-taker, presenter, data checker, manual author, and project support person.

This would be manageable if role transitions were explicit. In this case, the transitions appear to have been compressed and only partially scaffolded.

### Finding 4: Manufacturing ERP created extreme prerequisite load

Manufacturing ERP topics such as Item, BOM, Routing, Work Order, outsourced manufacturing, MRP, costing, and GL impact are not beginner topics.

They require staged explanation. When such topics are introduced early through customer artifacts and videos, the learner may fail not because of inability, but because the knowledge dependency graph is too dense.

### Finding 5: Data reconciliation was operational work, not simple training

Sales order upload, return process, and NetSuite-to-external-system comparison files required operational data quality judgment.

This is not ordinary onboarding reading. It is business process analysis.

### Finding 6: Artifact status was unclear

The materials included formal training files, customer documents, meeting notes, upload templates, videos, translation tasks, self-study notes, and access-related references.

Without artifact status labels, the learner cannot know what is official, what is draft, what is sensitive, what is customer-specific, and what is action-required.

### Finding 7: Security and access boundaries required stronger separation

Access-related or credential-adjacent artifacts should never be mixed with learning files or general project folders.

The case highlights the need for stricter separation between training, project data, and access governance.

### Finding 8: The case can be converted into CWI data

The most important outcome is that the experience can be converted into structured data.

A painful onboarding experience becomes analyzable when it is converted into event logs, artifact inventories, failure taxonomies, role matrices, knowledge-transfer gap matrices, and CWI interpretation.

## 9. CWI Interpretation

CWI means **Combinatorial Workflow Intelligence** in this project.

The basic idea is that organizational outcomes emerge from combinations of people, documents, systems, meetings, roles, permissions, data files, workflows, feedback loops, deadlines, and operational constraints.

The onboarding failure did not emerge from one variable alone. It emerged from the combination of many variables.

### 9.1 Context

The context was remote ERP consulting. The environment included live customers, implementation work, NetSuite training, manufacturing ERP artifacts, commerce integration, data reconciliation, translation, and self-study.

### 9.2 Work

The work was not only learning. It included observation, note-taking, translation, presentation, data checking, process interpretation, and documentation.

### 9.3 Impact

The impact included cognitive overload, role confusion, difficulty in determining learning priority, weak feedback visibility, and delayed recognition of the onboarding failure.

### 9.4 CWI core interpretation

The case can be summarized as follows:

> A remote ERP onboarding process became unstable because context, work, artifacts, role expectations, and feedback were not sufficiently staged into a coherent workflow.

## 10. Proposed Better Onboarding Design

A better onboarding design would have separated the experience into clear phases.

### Phase 1: ERP and NetSuite basics

ERP overview, record vs transaction, master data vs transaction data, NetSuite navigation, basic O2C/P2P flow, and glossary.

### Phase 2: Internal workflow orientation

Company project lifecycle, role definitions, meeting participation rules, documentation rules, artifact status labels, and escalation route.

### Phase 3: Customer context briefing

One-page customer profile, system landscape, project scope, process areas, known issues, and explicit learner boundaries.

### Phase 4: Guided sandbox practice

Item creation practice, sales order upload practice, return process simulation, BOM and Work Order sandbox exercise, and reconciliation exercise using synthetic data.

### Phase 5: Supervised project participation

Observe first, summarize next, support small tasks, receive feedback, and then handle narrow deliverables.

### Phase 6: Feedback and review

Weekly learning review, artifact review, role-boundary review, concept test, mentor comments, and next-week priority map.

## 11. SAP-Style CWI Extension

This case can be extended into a SAP-style CWI project.

The same logic can be applied to SAP or ERP event logs by analyzing process steps, role assignments, approval delays, data-quality exceptions, training gaps, master data failures, process deviations, and organizational bottlenecks.

This report has limitations.

## 14. Conclusion

This case study reframes a difficult remote ERP onboarding experience as a workflow and knowledge-transfer problem.

The key lesson is that onboarding does not happen simply because many materials exist.

Onboarding requires structure.

A new employee needs a staged pathway from basic concepts to live project participation. The organization must distinguish training artifacts from customer files, observation from responsibility, reference from action item, and self-study from supervised learning.

In this case, those boundaries were not sufficiently clear. The result was a compressed and fragmented onboarding environment.

However, the same experience can be recovered as research material.

By reconstructing the case as event logs, artifact inventories, failure taxonomies, role matrices, and knowledge-transfer gap matrices, the project turns a chaotic experience into a CWI foundation.

The final meaning of the case is therefore not only failure.

It is recovery.

It is a transformation from fragmented memory into structured operational intelligence.
