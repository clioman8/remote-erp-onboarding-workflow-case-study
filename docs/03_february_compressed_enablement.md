February: Compressed ERP Consultant Enablement
1. Summary

February represents the transition from live project exposure to compressed consultant enablement. During this period, the new employee encountered formal training materials, customer project artifacts, manufacturing ERP topics, presentation assignments, translation tasks, and self-study work at the same time.

This period shows that some training did exist. However, the training was not sufficiently separated from live delivery work.

The main problem was not total absence of education. The main problem was excessive compression.

2. Key Pattern

February materials suggest at least five parallel learning tracks.

Customer project track
Manufacturing and inventory track
D&D or delivery training track
O2C and financials presentation track
Translation and reference-material track

Each track may be meaningful on its own. However, when all tracks appear simultaneously in early onboarding, the learner faces knowledge overload.

3. Manufacturing ERP Overload

February introduced or reinforced manufacturing ERP topics such as Item, BOM, BOM Version, Work Order, Routing, Assembly Build, Costing Method, Backflush, GL Impact, and outsourced manufacturing.

These topics are not beginner-level ERP concepts.

They connect master data, inventory, manufacturing transactions, accounting impact, and customer-specific process design.

For onboarding purposes, these topics should have been modularized.

A better structure would have been:

Item master basics
Inventory and assembly item types
BOM basics
BOM revision and components
Work order basics
Routing and work center basics
Build and inventory impact
Costing and GL impact

Instead, the materials appear to have compressed several of these layers into the same learning period.

4. Formal Training Was Not Enough

Formal ERP or delivery training can provide product knowledge and implementation methodology. However, formal training does not automatically explain internal company workflows.

The learner still needs internal guidance.

The missing questions include:

Which customer project is the learner assigned to?
Which domain is the priority?
Which concepts are required now?
Which concepts can be postponed?
Which artifacts are for observation?
Which artifacts require action?
Who reviews the learner’s understanding?
What output is expected each week?

Without answers to these questions, formal training becomes another layer of workload rather than a stabilizing structure.

5. Presentation and Translation Pressure

February also included presentation-related and translation-related work. This is important because it indicates that the learner was not only absorbing information but also producing outputs.

Presentation work requires conceptual understanding, audience awareness, workflow explanation, and confidence in the material.

Translation work requires language processing, technical interpretation, and document-level comprehension.

When both are added to ERP learning, the onboarding burden increases.

6. Self-Regulated Learning Burden

The presence of self-study files, keyword lists, personal notes, and reconstructed study materials suggests that the employee had to build a private learning system.

This is not necessarily bad. Self-directed learning is valuable.

However, when self-directed learning replaces structured onboarding, the burden shifts from the organization to the individual.

The employee becomes responsible not only for learning the content, but also for designing the learning pathway.

7. CWI Interpretation

From a CWI perspective, February represents a failure of learning-path segmentation.

The organization had many artifacts and activities, but the employee lacked a clear staged workflow for moving from beginner understanding to productive participation.

The key issue was not whether education existed. The key issue was whether education, practice, delivery, feedback, and role expectations were aligned.

8. Key Finding

February shows that compressed remote onboarding can become overwhelming when formal training, live project artifacts, presentation work, translation work, and manufacturing ERP topics are delivered in parallel without a clear learning hierarchy.

This period should be interpreted as compressed ERP consultant enablement rather than standard new-employee onboarding.
